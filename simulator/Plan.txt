Analyzing the Simulator Options
For academic theses involving IoT, evaluators look for two types of validation: Hardware Constrained Performance (does it run fast enough on a weak CPU?) and Network Scalability (does the blockchain handle 100 concurrent devices?).

Here are your options:

Option 1: True ARM Hardware Emulation (The QEMU approach)
Using critoma/qemu-rpi-os-lite as you suggested.

What it does: Runs a full emulation of a Raspberry Pi ARM processor inside Docker.
Pros: Highly realistic hardware validation. You can proudly state in your Thesis that the ECDSA secp256r1 cryptography was tested on an emulated ARM environment, not just your fast PC processor.
Cons: Very heavy and slow. Booting the QEMU OS takes time, and you cannot easily run 50 of these at once on a laptop without freezing your PC.
Option 2: Lightweight Application Containers (The Scalability approach)
Using node:18-alpine or standard minimal Docker containers.

What it does: Packages your device.js script into a lightweight 50MB Docker container mapping directly to your PC's CPU.
Pros: Blazing fast. We can write a docker-compose.yml file to instantly launch 50, 100, or 200 virtual sensors at the exact same time to stress-test your Hyperledger Fabric backend.
Cons: It uses your PC's x86 processor speed, so the cryptographic latency metrics will be faster than what a real cheap IoT sensor would achieve.
Option 3: The Hybrid Approach (🏆 Highly Recommended)
Why not both? This makes for an incredibly strong Thesis chapter.

We use ONE QEMU ARM container to take realistic cryptographic latency measurements (e.g., "Registration takes 400ms on an emulated Raspberry Pi").
We use a Docker Compose swarm of 50 lightweight Node.js containers to test the blockchain's throughput under concurrent load.
